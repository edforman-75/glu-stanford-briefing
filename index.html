GLU Operating Model

Briefing Package for Stanford GSB Project Team — February 2026
What is this? GLU is an AI-native Product Information Management company. It diagnoses why AI assistants (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Google AI) can't recommend a brand's products — and provides specifications to fix it. This package presents two competing business model alternatives for your analysis.

What GLU Does

When a consumer asks ChatGPT "What's the best bamboo hoodie for running?", the AI searches across data sources to form a recommendation. If a brand's product data is incomplete, inconsistent, or missing key signals, the AI can't recommend them — even if the product is excellent.

GLU diagnoses these gaps and produces detailed specifications for fixing them. The company calls this AI Engine Optimization (AEO) — making product information machine-readable so AI systems can accurately recommend products.

GLU's founders face a fundamental choice between two business models that look similar on the surface but have radically different implications for how the company operates, scales, and creates value:

Model Comparison

Specialist Services (Mu Sigma) vs. Diagnostic Intelligence (Operating Model)

Model A: Specialist Services — "Mu Sigma for AEO"

Description

GLU operates as an AEO consulting firm built on the Mu Sigma archetype: proprietary methodology delivered through specialist leverage. Senior diagnosticians develop analytical frameworks; junior specialists execute bounded diagnostic tasks under supervision. The company scales by hiring specialists faster than revenue grows (specialist leverage ratio of 1:3).

Key insight: This is a people business that uses technology as enablement. Revenue scales with headcount. The advantage is accumulated institutional knowledge and proprietary methodology, delivered by trained humans.

Customer Journey

Awareness: Brand hears about AEO through conference, agency partner, or case study. Discovery: Founder-led consultation call, diagnostic sample. The Mu Sigma model scales through specialist leverage: for every senior diagnostician ($120K–$150K), hire 3 junior specialists ($40K–$55K each, Pune-based). Seniors define frameworks and quality standards; juniors execute bounded diagnostic tasks. Each senior + 3 juniors can handle ~$800K–$1.2M in annual engagement revenue.

Model B: Operating Model — Diagnostic Intelligence Company

Description

GLU operates as a product company that sells diagnostic intelligence delivered by software. The diagnostic engine accumulates knowledge (SSOT — Single Source of Truth) across every engagement, making each subsequent diagnosis faster and more accurate. The company stays small by design because software does the work, not people.

Key insight: This is a software business that uses people for governance and improvement. Revenue scales with software utilization. The advantage is accumulated diagnostic knowledge that compounds through use, delivered by automated systems with human oversight.

The 7 Core Objectives

  1. We sell diagnosis, not execution — Customers pay for understanding, not implementation
  2. We protect IP through compartmentalization — Apple archetype, black-box methods
  3. We grow through accumulated knowledge, not headcount — SSOT accretion is the scaling mechanism
  4. Partners sell for us — Agency leverage replaces sales headcount
  5. We're a product company, not a service company — Automation first, no coaching or consulting
  6. We reach breakeven fast with minimal overhead — Month 8 on $250K starting cash
  7. We build an attractive business — High margins, defensible IP, clear unit economics

Key Questions for Your Analysis

  1. Diagnosis-only vs. diagnosis+execution: The Mu Sigma model sells both. The Operating Model explicitly rejects execution (except bounded Deploy). Is pure diagnosis viable, or will customers demand implementation?
  2. SSOT accretion: The Operating Model bets on knowledge compounding as the scaling mechanism. What evidence would validate this? How do you test it before committing?
  3. Hybrid path: Could GLU start with Operating Model economics and add Mu Sigma-style execution tiers later? Or do the operating principles prohibit this?
  4. Async-first: The Operating Model enforces no standing meetings, no account management, email-first communication. The Mu Sigma model has regular client touchpoints. Which is realistic for $10K+ engagements?
  5. Margin differential: Operating Model shows 87–99% gross margins with $5–25 delivery cost. Mu Sigma shows 60–70%. Is the Operating Model's margin projection credible?
  6. LTV:CAC comparison: Operating Model is 39:1 (App Store channel) vs. Mu Sigma's 10:1–25:1. What drives the gap? Which is more defensible?
  7. Capital requirements: The Operating Model reaches breakeven at Month 8 on $250K. The Mu Sigma model needs $500K–$1M to fund hiring ramp. What does each require in terms of risk tolerance?
  8. Distribution dependency: Both models depend on agency partnerships. But the Operating Model also relies on Shopify App Store (Risk #4: existential if rejected). How material is this?
  9. Pricing anchor: Operating Model prices Analyze at $3,500 (sold direct). Mu Sigma's AEO Audit starts at $12,000. Same diagnostic, very different framing. What does this signal?
  10. Bot scraping risk: The Operating Model's highest risk is chatbot bot scraping failure, which would collapse margins from 93% to 50–85%. The Mu Sigma model is less exposed because humans do the work. How do you weigh this?
  11. Market validation: Shopify's "Agentic Storefronts" announcement validates the market thesis. But does market validation favor the product model (scale quickly) or the services model (capture premium value)?
  12. Exit implications: Services businesses trade at 1–3× revenue. Product businesses trade at 8–15×. Does the Operating Model's product positioning hold up under diligence, or will investors see it as services in disguise?

Operating Principles

How We Make Decisions and What Stays Protected
Core Principle: We are driven by customer pull and research evidence, not unsupported opinion. Our roadmap follows clear signals from customers and rigorous research. We protect our competitive advantage by keeping methods confidential while delivering exceptional results.

Principle 1: Customer-Driven Roadmap

We Build What Customers Need, Not What We Think They Need

GLU's product roadmap is driven by customer pull and research evidence, not by unsupported opinion, competitor mimicry, or founder intuition alone.

What Qualifies as a Valid Signal

Direct customer requests: Multiple customers asking for the same feature, willingness to pay more, or declining engagement because of missing capability.

Research-driven insights: Data showing a platform's influence on AI recommendations (e.g., Amazon's extraordinary influence on chatbot responses), analysis revealing critical gaps, or competitive research showing missed opportunities.

Partner feedback: Agency partners reporting lost deals due to missing platform support, or requesting specific diagnostic capabilities.

What Does NOT Qualify

  • ✗ "Everyone's doing X" — Competitor mimicry without customer evidence
  • ✗ "I think customers would like Y" — Founder opinion without validation
  • ✗ "This would be cool to build" — Engineering-driven features
  • ✗ "One customer asked for Z" — Isolated request without pattern

Roadmap Governance Rule

Before adding any feature to the roadmap, answer four questions with evidence: (1) How many customers are asking? (2) What research supports this? (3) Does this make us better at providing advice, or just broader? (4) Can we deliver without compromising IP protection?

What We DO Share

Findings (what's broken), Recommendations (specific actions), Evidence (examples of the problem), Deployment drafts (AI-generated content), and Monitoring reports (ongoing tracking).

Key distinction: We tell customers what to change and why it matters. We don't tell them how we figured it out.

How We Protect Methods

Partners get results and specifications, not methodology. They cannot access our diagnostic engine, get training on methods, run diagnostics themselves, or white-label.

Contractors execute bounded tasks without understanding the full system. Task-specific instructions only, no access to full codebase, NDA coverage.

If a customer insists on understanding methods, they're not the right customer. Our value is in delivering results, not transferring knowledge.

We Sell Diagnosis, Not Execution

Product Boundaries That Protect Our Business Model
Core Principle: Customers pay us to understand what's broken, not to fix it for them. We provide clarity, evidence, and detailed specifications. We don't become an agency that implements changes.

Why this matters: Execution services scale linearly with headcount and erode margins. Diagnostic intelligence scales through accumulated knowledge and compounds over time.

Observe and Analyze are pure diagnosis — the core product. Deploy is bounded execution with structural protections. Monitor is automated observation.

Deploy Phase Guardrails

Deploy is the only phase where we cross into execution. Structural protections prevent agency drift:

  • Optional — Never required. Customers can apply specs themselves.
  • Requires diagnosis first — No Deploy without completed Observe + Analyze.
  • Add-only — Cannot delete, overwrite, or reposition existing content.
  • Does not interpret — Ambiguity escalates to human review. Mechanical application only.
  • Evaluated on fidelity, not outcomes — Did we apply exactly what was specified?
We apply templates. We are not creative. Our software executes patterns and applies templates at scale. We don't invent new brand concepts, create campaign strategies, or make creative decisions. If it requires creative judgment, that's human work. If it's applying a template systematically, that's our work.

Boundaries with Other Providers

Content agencies: We provide diagnostically-grounded specifications. They write prose in brand voice, create images, design layouts.

SEO/growth agencies: We tell them what is true about platform influence. They decide strategy based on that truth.

Software platforms: Platforms provide capabilities. We provide intelligence about whether those capabilities produce coherent results.

Operations providers: They apply what we specified. They don't diagnose or create specifications.

When to Say No

  • Customer wants execution without diagnosis
  • Customer wants creative/strategic content
  • Customer wants ongoing operational management
  • Customer wants experimentation ("figure out what works")
  • Request requires creative judgment or strategic innovation

Operating Model Archetypes

How Different Companies Create and Protect Advantage

This framework explains three fundamental approaches to building competitive advantage. These aren't models to copy — they're patterns that work for specific business types.

The Three Archetypes

Apple Archetype: Coherence & Judgment

Core optimization: Coherence, restraint, compartmentalization

Extreme compartmentalization (teams don't know what other teams build), silence and restraint (no premature announcements), judgment over process (senior authority makes final calls), trust via consistency (products work together seamlessly).

Why it works for Apple: Product differentiation is everything. If competitors knew Apple's roadmap 12 months early, it would destroy their advantage. Ecosystem lock-in, premium margins, and small product line enable tight control.

Google Archetype: Learning Velocity & Publication

Core optimization: Learning speed, research credibility, ecosystem building

External publication (research papers, open source), transparency as recruiting (top researchers want to publish), ecosystem building (Android, TensorFlow become platforms others build on).

Why it works for Google: Network effects, advertising business model (open-sourcing doesn't hurt ad revenue), talent acquisition, and data advantages (competitors can't copy Google's data even with Google's algorithms).

Amazon Archetype: Execution Velocity & Throughput

Core optimization: Execution speed, operational excellence, scale efficiency

Radical internal transparency (everyone sees metrics), process documentation (everything written, repeatable, scalable), metrics-driven operations, trust via throughput.

Why it works for Amazon: Logistics and execution ARE the advantage. Competitors know Amazon's strategy but can't replicate the execution. Scale economics make volume = lower unit costs.

Mu Sigma as Reference Point

Mu Sigma ($1.5B+ analytics company) proves the Apple archetype works for services/analytics businesses. They use proprietary methodology not disclosed to clients or employees, compartmentalized teams, and "decision theaters" for structured delivery.

Key difference from GLU: Mu Sigma = people deliver (analysts do the work for each client → scales to thousands of employees). GLU = software delivers (AI generates analysis → stays small, same software runs more times). Both use Apple archetype for IP protection, but fundamentally different economics.

Why GLU Needs Apple Archetype

  1. Accumulated diagnostic knowledge is the advantage — Publishing methods or training contractors on the full system creates competitors.
  2. Small scale by design — Centralized judgment and coherence work at this scale. Amazon's transparency works for massive scale; GLU won't reach that.
  3. Customer trust comes from consistency, not methodology — Customers trust the diagnosis is right without seeing under the hood.
  4. Product (diagnostic output), not service (execution) — Publishing would commoditize the diagnostic capability.

What Would Happen with Wrong Archetype?

If Google (Publication): Agencies copy diagnostic frameworks → pricing power erodes → accumulated knowledge advantage disappears.

If Amazon (Radical Transparency): Contractors understand full system → people leave and start competing companies → IP leaks through training materials.

Operating Plan Risks & Mitigations

Threats to the Financial Model and Required Amendments
Risk Framework: This financial model assumes software can deliver diagnostics at scale ($5–25 cost) with minimal human labor. The risks below could fundamentally change the economics.

Risk #1: Bot Scraping Failure Highest Impact

Risk: Unable to use bots to extract data from AI chatbots. Would require human operators ($200–500/diagnostic vs. $5–10 automated).

Impact: Gross margin collapse 93% → 50–85%. CAC $252 → $6,700+. LTV:CAC 39:1 → 1.5:1.

Mitigations: Accept lower margins with Pune contractors (+$50/diagnostic, margin stays 83%). Price increase to $4,500. Reduce chatbot coverage (2 platforms vs. 5). Chatbot-lite tier at $2,500.

Amendment needed: +$100K starting cash ($350K total) to handle higher variable costs.

Risk #2: Unit Costs Increase Low Risk

Even 5× cost increase only reduces margins from 93% to 85% because compute/labor is tiny relative to revenue ($10–25 vs. $3,500–$12,000). No amendment required.

Risk #3: Conversion Rates Below Projections Moderate

If App Store → Analyze drops to 1%, CAC rises to $600 but LTV:CAC remains 17:1. Breakeven delays to Month 9. No amendment required.

Risk #4: Shopify App Store Rejection Existential

Without App Store presence, cannot execute Deploy on Shopify stores. LTV collapses $9,950 → $3,500. LTV:CAC drops to 14:1.

Mitigations: Pre-submission compliance with Shopify Partner Success, legal review, proactive customer support, privacy-first data handling.

Customer Journey Walkthrough

Full Customer Journey: Observe → Analyze → Deploy → Monitor

Example Customer: TASC Performance (athletic apparel, 919 products)

Operating Model Context: GLU follows the Apple archetype — coherence, judgment, restraint. No ROI promises. Human judgment required. Product, not service. Prohibited language includes "optimize", "guarantee", "boost revenue". Approved language: "clarify", "align", "verify", "correct", "improved advice".

Phase 1: Observe

Diagnostic: What's Broken

Platform analyzes current state of AI visibility. Customer gets: gap analysis, competitive reality, query volume (~18K monthly searches where TASC should appear), root cause analysis.

TASC findings: 919 products with systematic gaps — 98% missing material composition, 98% missing care instructions, 99% missing sustainability messaging. AI can't answer "What's this made of?" or "How do I care for bamboo fabric?"

Read-only access only. No content changes. Pure diagnostic.

Phase 2: Analyze

Prescription: What to Change

Shows what needs fixing + creates urgency. Customer gets: technical plan, competitive framing (Lululemon, Vuori, Patagonia winning queries), urgency, effort estimates (1,215 hours manual vs. 4–5 weeks with GLU).

TASC result: 6,824 changes needed across product titles, descriptions, metafields, site pages, schema markup, and robots.txt.

Critical boundary: No ROI claims. We don't promise "$5M revenue increase." We frame urgency around competitive positioning, category ownership, and time window for early movers.

Phase 3: Deploy

Execution: Content Drafts for Approval

Platform generates, human reviews, human decides. Customer gets AI-generated drafts with side-by-side before/after comparison. Every change requires explicit human approval (Accept/Edit/Reject). Rolling deployment at customer's pace.

Example: "Carrollton Lightweight Hoodie" → "Carrollton Bamboo Performance Hoodie — Moisture-Wicking Activewear for Men"

Deployment options: API (auto) if write permissions granted, CSV export if no write permissions.
